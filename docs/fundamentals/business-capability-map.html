Business Capability Map

A working inventory of the recurring capabilities a business must solve. The map names outcomes, not departments. A capability can be performed by an owner, employee, agent, software system, vendor, partner, franchise parent, or some combination of them.

Format: HTML-native Authoring: edit this HTML file Status: Draft working map

How to read this

A business is an intent-driven system for building value, delivering value, and capturing money. Most businesses therefore share a recurring capability shape. They still differ in market, model, product, constraints, scale, regulation, channels, people, and tools.

A capability is solved when the business can reliably produce the named outcome under real constraints. A job is the named body of work required for that capability to exist, operate, or improve. A process is the repeatable sequence that performs the job. The actor can change; the capability still has to be handled.

This map is meant for intake and diagnosis. When entering a business, ask how each capability is solved today, what jobs make it real, who or what performs those jobs, what process is followed, what context is required, what tools are used, and what evidence proves that the outcome happened.

The broader operating concept lives in Visible Business Foundation: Foundation should make the business inspectable, not merely documented.

Operating rule: Classify the work by the capability it supports before choosing the actor. Payroll can be a person, vendor, software workflow, or agent-assisted system. The durable capability is still paying people accurately and on time.

Top-level map

The highest-level business capability is to build value, deliver value, and capture money while keeping the system able to learn. The map below groups the recurring capability families that support that goal.

01
Direct the business
  • Choose goals
  • Set priorities
  • Allocate resources
  • Hold accountability
02
Build demand and value
  • Understand the market
  • Design the offer
  • Create or source value
  • Generate trust and attention
03
Deliver and support
  • Convert demand
  • Fulfill the promise
  • Support customers
  • Retain and expand
04
Operate and protect
  • Run workflows
  • Manage people and partners
  • Control money
  • Handle risk and compliance
05
Learn and improve
  • Collect evidence
  • Evaluate performance
  • Update context
  • Revise the system

The categories are practical, not sacred. Some businesses combine capabilities in one role. Others split a single capability across many teams. The map is useful when it lets an entrepreneur see what must be true, what is currently true, and where the next improvement should happen.

Direction

Direction capabilities decide what the business is trying to produce, where resources go, and how tradeoffs are resolved.

01

Goal and strategy system

The business can choose the outcomes that matter and translate them into priorities.

where work points

Capability outcome: the business has a clear goal, a chosen market, a strategy for winning, and a small enough set of priorities that people and systems can act.

Typical jobs
  • Define the business goal, economic target, and customer outcome.
  • Choose the market, customer segment, and competitive position.
  • Set quarterly or operating-period priorities.
  • Translate strategy into capability gaps and jobs.
Evidence
  • Written goals and decision criteria.
  • Priority list tied to owner, deadline, and metric.
  • Tradeoffs resolved without repeated re-litigation.
02

Governance and decision system

The business can make, record, delegate, and revise decisions.

how choices happen

Capability outcome: important choices have owners, decision rights, inputs, constraints, and review points.

Typical jobs
  • Define decision rights and approval thresholds.
  • Run operating reviews and escalation meetings.
  • Record decisions, rationale, and expected evidence.
  • Review decisions when reality changes.
Evidence
  • Decision log or operating record.
  • Clear approval paths.
  • Fewer blocked jobs and repeated debates.
03

Resource allocation

The business can assign time, people, money, attention, and tools to the work that matters.

where capacity goes

Capability outcome: the business can choose what gets funded, staffed, automated, outsourced, paused, or stopped.

Typical jobs
  • Plan capacity against goals and constraints.
  • Budget labor, tools, inventory, marketing, and operations.
  • Assign accountable owners to capabilities and jobs.
  • Review opportunity cost and reallocate when evidence changes.
Evidence
  • Budget, staffing, and capacity plan.
  • Work queued by priority, not noise.
  • Spending and effort tied to expected outcomes.

Build value

Build-value capabilities define who the business serves, what problem it solves, what promise it makes, and what product or service can make that promise true.

04

Market and customer understanding

The business can understand the customer, the problem, the buying context, and the alternatives.

what is valuable

Capability outcome: the business knows who it serves, what those customers value, what pain or desire drives action, and what would count as a better outcome.

Typical jobs
  • Interview customers, prospects, and lost buyers.
  • Map segments, use cases, pains, objections, and buying triggers.
  • Study competitors, substitutes, category norms, and market constraints.
  • Convert customer evidence into offer, product, and messaging updates.
Evidence
  • Customer definitions and segment notes.
  • Observed buying behavior.
  • Win/loss reasons and recurring objections.
05

Positioning and category clarity

The business can explain what it is, who it is for, and why it is the right choice.

why this choice

Capability outcome: customers, employees, partners, and operators understand the business's category, promise, difference, and proof.

Typical jobs
  • Name the category and customer problem.
  • Define the promise, contrast, proof, and reason to believe.
  • Translate positioning into website, sales, recruiting, and partner language.
  • Test whether the market understands the message.
Evidence
  • Clear positioning statement.
  • Higher qualified response from target buyers.
  • Consistent language across interfaces.
06

Offer design

The business can package value into a promise customers understand and want.

what is promised

Capability outcome: the business has an offer with a named customer, outcome, scope, price, delivery model, proof, guarantee or risk reversal, and constraints.

Typical jobs
  • Define the offer promise, customer, outcome, inclusions, and exclusions.
  • Design packaging, bundles, service levels, subscriptions, or menus.
  • Set proof, guarantee, urgency, scarcity, and qualification rules.
  • Review whether fulfillment can make the offer true.
Evidence
  • Offer spec or catalog entry.
  • Conversion and fulfillment quality.
  • Customer comprehension and reduced mismatch.
07

Product or service creation and sourcing

The business can create, acquire, assemble, or access what it sells.

what creates value

Capability outcome: the business can reliably produce or source the product, service, assortment, inventory, expertise, or operational result required by the offer.

Typical jobs
  • Design, build, source, license, or partner for the thing sold.
  • Maintain assortment, catalog, service menu, or product roadmap.
  • Verify availability, fit, margin, quality, and constraints.
  • Update products or services when demand, cost, supply, or evidence changes.
Evidence
  • Product or service catalog.
  • Availability and margin data.
  • Customer outcomes and defect signals.
08

Quality and value standards

The business can define and maintain the standard that makes the promise true.

what good means

Capability outcome: quality is defined, observable, measured, and maintained across product, service, support, and operations.

Typical jobs
  • Define quality criteria, acceptance criteria, and failure modes.
  • Inspect product, service, delivery, and customer experience.
  • Run quality gates, reviews, tests, and corrective actions.
  • Convert defects and complaints into process improvements.
Evidence
  • Quality checklist or eval spec.
  • Defect, return, complaint, and rework data.
  • Standards visible to actors doing the work.

Reach and sell

Reach-and-sell capabilities make the offer visible, credible, available, and purchasable by the right buyers.

09

Brand, trust, and reputation

The business can earn enough trust for customers, partners, and employees to engage.

why trust exists

Capability outcome: the market sees credible proof, consistent identity, positive reputation, and confidence that the business will do what it says.

Typical jobs
  • Maintain identity, reputation, reviews, testimonials, and public proof.
  • Publish credible assets, case studies, before-and-after examples, or certifications.
  • Respond to reputation events, complaints, and public risk.
  • Keep brand language consistent across buyer and employee interfaces.
Evidence
  • Review profile, referrals, and brand search.
  • Trust signals used in sales and hiring.
  • Reputation recovery when issues happen.
10

Distribution and market access

The business can make the offer reachable and available to the right market.

where buyers find it

Capability outcome: buyers can discover, access, consider, and buy the offer through channels that fit the business model.

Typical jobs
  • Choose channels: location, search, marketplace, partner, outbound, retail, distributor, direct sales, content, or referrals.
  • Maintain channel presence, availability, listings, routing, and partner rules.
  • Measure reach, qualified attention, channel cost, and channel reliability.
  • Improve channels when the offer is valuable but hidden.
Evidence
  • Traffic, footfall, impressions, referrals, or qualified leads.
  • Channel performance by cost and conversion.
  • Offer availability where buyers expect it.
11

Demand generation

The business can create enough qualified attention for the offer.

how attention starts

Capability outcome: the business can repeatedly generate interest from people or organizations likely to buy.

Typical jobs
  • Plan campaigns, content, ads, events, outbound, partnerships, or local promotion.
  • Create messages and assets tied to the offer and segment.
  • Run lead capture, qualification, attribution, and follow-up.
  • Improve demand generation based on cost, quality, and sales outcomes.
Evidence
  • Qualified pipeline or purchase intent.
  • Cost per qualified opportunity.
  • Demand sources tied to actual revenue.
12

Sales conversion

The business can turn qualified demand into a buying decision.

how buyers say yes

Capability outcome: qualified buyers can understand the offer, resolve objections, approve terms, and purchase with low friction.

Typical jobs
  • Qualify buyers and route opportunities.
  • Present the offer, answer objections, quote, negotiate, and close.
  • Prepare contracts, order forms, proposals, carts, or checkout flows.
  • Inspect win rate, sales cycle, lost reasons, and handoff quality.
Evidence
  • Conversion rate and close rate.
  • Sales notes, call quality, and lost reasons.
  • Signed agreements, orders, or completed checkouts.
13

Pricing and commercial terms

The business can set prices, payment terms, and packaging that capture enough value.

how value is priced

Capability outcome: prices, margins, payment terms, discounts, financing, renewals, and guarantees support the business model and customer decision.

Typical jobs
  • Set list price, discount rules, payment terms, and renewal terms.
  • Calculate margin, unit economics, payback, and cash impact.
  • Maintain price books, menus, quotes, and billing rules.
  • Test pricing against conversion, retention, delivery cost, and profit.
Evidence
  • Gross margin and contribution margin.
  • Average order value or contract value.
  • Discount leakage and pricing exceptions.

Deliver value

Delivery capabilities make the promise true after the customer says yes.

14

Order intake and onboarding

The business can convert a sale into the context and setup required to deliver.

how work begins

Capability outcome: every new customer, order, job, or account starts with the right information, expectations, permissions, schedule, and handoff.

Typical jobs
  • Collect requirements, customer data, payment details, and constraints.
  • Confirm scope, expectations, timeline, and responsibilities.
  • Set up accounts, records, access, tickets, work orders, or project spaces.
  • Route handoffs from sales to fulfillment, support, or operations.
Evidence
  • Complete intake records.
  • Clean handoffs with fewer missing details.
  • Customer readiness and reduced rework.
15

Fulfillment and delivery

The business can deliver the promised product, service, or result reliably.

how value arrives

Capability outcome: customers receive what was promised at the expected quality, time, cost, and scope.

Typical jobs
  • Schedule, prepare, produce, pick, pack, ship, install, implement, perform, or complete the work.
  • Coordinate handoffs, dependencies, inventory, capacity, and exceptions.
  • Verify completion against the offer and quality standard.
  • Communicate delivery state to customers and internal owners.
Evidence
  • Delivered orders or completed services.
  • On-time, in-full, and quality metrics.
  • Completion proof and customer acceptance.
16

Customer support and issue resolution

The business can help customers when something is unclear, broken, late, or incomplete.

how problems close

Capability outcome: customer problems are received, understood, routed, resolved, and converted into operating evidence.

Typical jobs
  • Receive support requests, complaints, returns, claims, and exceptions.
  • Triage urgency, ownership, root cause, and resolution path.
  • Communicate status, resolve the issue, and confirm customer acceptance.
  • Update products, processes, docs, training, or policies from support patterns.
Evidence
  • Resolution time and backlog.
  • Complaint, return, refund, and escalation patterns.
  • Support causes linked to system updates.
17

Retention and expansion

The business can keep valuable customers and grow the relationship when more value can be delivered.

how value compounds

Capability outcome: customers continue, renew, repurchase, refer, expand, or remain satisfied when the business model depends on ongoing value.

Typical jobs
  • Monitor customer health, satisfaction, usage, outcomes, and renewal risk.
  • Run renewal, reorder, subscription, account review, and expansion workflows.
  • Identify unmet needs and appropriate next offers.
  • Recover at-risk customers when the relationship is still valuable.
Evidence
  • Retention, churn, repeat purchase, and expansion data.
  • Customer outcome evidence.
  • Referral and account growth signals.

Capture and control money

Money-control capabilities turn value into cash, keep the business solvent, and make the economics visible.

18

Billing, payment, and collections

The business can invoice, charge, collect, reconcile, and resolve payment exceptions.

how money arrives

Capability outcome: earned revenue becomes collected cash with clear records and low leakage.

Typical jobs
  • Create invoices, subscriptions, payment links, checkout flows, or charge records.
  • Collect payments, handle failed charges, and follow up on receivables.
  • Apply payments to customer records and orders.
  • Resolve disputes, credits, refunds, and write-offs.
Evidence
  • Collected cash and aging receivables.
  • Payment failure and refund rates.
  • Revenue records matched to delivery and bank activity.
19

Finance, accounting, and cash control

The business can know where money is, where it is going, and whether the model works.

what the numbers say

Capability outcome: financial records, cash, profit, obligations, unit economics, and forecasts are accurate enough to guide decisions.

Typical jobs
  • Book transactions, reconcile accounts, and close financial periods.
  • Track revenue, cost of goods, labor, overhead, margin, taxes, debt, and cash.
  • Forecast cash flow, budgets, and unit economics.
  • Prepare financial reports for decisions, taxes, lenders, owners, or investors.
Evidence
  • Reconciled books and financial statements.
  • Cash forecast and budget variance.
  • Profitability and unit economics by offer or channel.
20

Tax, reporting, and financial obligations

The business can meet recurring financial obligations accurately and on time.

what must be paid

Capability outcome: required filings, taxes, debt payments, owner distributions, vendor payments, and reporting obligations are known, scheduled, paid, and evidenced.

Typical jobs
  • Maintain tax calendar, reporting requirements, and payment schedules.
  • Prepare sales tax, income tax, payroll tax, excise tax, or domain-specific filings.
  • Manage debt, lease, insurance, license, and contract payment obligations.
  • Keep proof of filings, payments, and approvals.
Evidence
  • Filed returns and payment confirmations.
  • Obligation calendar.
  • Low penalty, interest, and missed-payment risk.

Operate the system

Operating capabilities make work repeatable. They define jobs, processes, actors, tools, context, and handoffs.

21

Process and workflow management

The business can turn recurring work into repeatable paths.

how work repeats

Capability outcome: recurring jobs have clear steps, decisions, checks, handoffs, owners, tools, and evidence.

Typical jobs
  • Map current workflows, interfaces, handoffs, decisions, and failure points.
  • Create SOPs, checklists, skills, automations, and workflow definitions.
  • Assign process owners and quality gates.
  • Improve process clarity when outcomes miss.
Evidence
  • Process maps and SOPs used in real work.
  • Reduced rework, waiting, misses, and ambiguity.
  • Process evidence captured at the point of work.
22

People, roles, and job ownership

The business can attract, select, onboard, train, assign, and manage the people needed for the work.

who owns work

Capability outcome: roles and titles are decomposed into jobs, expectations, skills, accountabilities, capacity, and performance evidence.

Typical jobs
  • Define roles, job responsibilities, hiring criteria, and scorecards.
  • Recruit, select, onboard, train, coach, and review people.
  • Assign jobs and resolve capacity, accountability, and ownership gaps.
  • Manage performance, compensation expectations, and role changes.
Evidence
  • Job descriptions tied to capabilities.
  • Hiring pipeline and onboarding completion.
  • Performance evidence and ownership clarity.
23

Payroll and workforce administration

The business can pay people accurately, on time, and with the right records.

how people are paid

Capability outcome: time, pay, taxes, benefits, classifications, approvals, and payroll records are handled reliably.

Typical jobs
  • Collect time, salary, commission, bonus, deduction, and benefit inputs.
  • Approve hours, calculate pay, run payroll, and submit taxes or filings.
  • Resolve payroll exceptions, corrections, reimbursements, and off-cycle payments.
  • Maintain employee records, classifications, policies, and pay evidence.
Evidence
  • Payroll run confirmations.
  • Accurate employee pay and tax records.
  • Low correction, complaint, and compliance risk.
24

Procurement and supplier management

The business can acquire the goods, services, software, and inputs required to operate and fulfill.

what inputs arrive

Capability outcome: required inputs are sourced, purchased, received, paid for, and reviewed against quality, cost, risk, and availability.

Typical jobs
  • Select suppliers, vendors, tools, contractors, agencies, distributors, and partners.
  • Negotiate terms, place orders, track delivery, and approve invoices.
  • Manage substitutions, shortages, lead times, returns, and vendor issues.
  • Review supplier performance and concentration risk.
Evidence
  • Purchase orders, contracts, and vendor records.
  • Cost, quality, lead time, and issue history.
  • Inputs available when the work requires them.
25

Inventory, assets, and facilities

The business can maintain the physical and digital assets required for work.

what must be ready

Capability outcome: inventory, equipment, locations, vehicles, devices, credentials, and other assets are available, secure, maintained, and fit for use.

Typical jobs
  • Track inventory levels, reorder points, shrink, storage, and movement.
  • Maintain facilities, equipment, vehicles, fixtures, devices, and access.
  • Schedule repairs, inspections, replenishment, and preventive maintenance.
  • Record asset ownership, status, cost, warranty, and replacement needs.
Evidence
  • Inventory accuracy and availability.
  • Maintenance records and uptime.
  • Lower loss, downtime, and stockout risk.
26

Technology, tooling, and automation

The business can choose, run, integrate, and improve the tools that perform or support work.

what actors use

Capability outcome: software, hardware, agents, automations, integrations, data flows, and access systems make work faster, clearer, safer, or more reliable.

Typical jobs
  • Select tools based on capability gaps and actor needs.
  • Implement systems, integrations, automations, permissions, and monitoring.
  • Maintain reliability, backups, security, and support.
  • Retire or replace tools that create drag or hide reality.
Evidence
  • Tool inventory and ownership.
  • Workflow speed, error reduction, and data quality.
  • System uptime, access logs, and support volume.

Protect the system

Protection capabilities keep the business within the constraints that let it keep operating.

27

Legal, compliance, and regulatory control

The business can understand and meet the obligations that govern its work.

what must be true

Capability outcome: licenses, permits, contracts, policies, labor rules, privacy obligations, safety requirements, and industry regulations are known, assigned, monitored, and evidenced.

Typical jobs
  • Identify applicable laws, permits, licenses, filings, policies, and contract obligations.
  • Maintain compliance calendars, records, approvals, and owner assignments.
  • Review contracts, vendor terms, employment obligations, and customer commitments.
  • Respond to audits, incidents, claims, inspections, and regulatory changes.
Evidence
  • Compliance register and proof records.
  • Completed filings, licenses, audits, and renewals.
  • Reduced penalty, dispute, and shutdown risk.
28

Risk, safety, and continuity

The business can anticipate, reduce, absorb, and recover from material risks.

how failure is handled

Capability outcome: key risks are named, owners are assigned, controls exist, incidents are handled, and critical work can continue or recover.

Typical jobs
  • Identify operational, financial, safety, security, supply, reputational, and strategic risks.
  • Maintain insurance, safety procedures, incident response, backups, and continuity plans.
  • Train actors on critical controls and escalation paths.
  • Review incidents and near misses into system updates.
Evidence
  • Risk register and mitigation owners.
  • Incident, safety, downtime, and near-miss data.
  • Recovery tests and continuity readiness.
29

Security, access, and control of sensitive assets

The business can protect money, data, systems, facilities, and customer trust.

what is protected

Capability outcome: sensitive assets have clear owners, access rules, monitoring, response paths, and evidence of control.

Typical jobs
  • Define access rules for money, systems, data, locations, devices, and customer records.
  • Provision, review, revoke, and monitor access.
  • Protect credentials, payments, private data, inventory, and facilities.
  • Investigate exceptions, fraud, breaches, theft, and policy violations.
Evidence
  • Access logs, permissions, and review records.
  • Incident and exception reports.
  • Controls aligned with asset sensitivity.

Learn and improve

Learning capabilities make the business smarter by turning evidence into revised artifacts, decisions, processes, and capabilities.

30

Data, measurement, and evaluation

The business can know whether actions, processes, jobs, and capabilities worked.

how reality answers

Capability outcome: the business collects reliable evidence and evaluates it against intent often enough to change decisions.

Typical jobs
  • Define metrics, eval specs, dashboards, reports, scorecards, and quality gates.
  • Collect data from sales, operations, finance, support, product, people, and tools.
  • Judge evidence against goals, standards, and expected outcomes.
  • Route evaluation results to owners who can update the system.
Evidence
  • Metrics tied to decisions.
  • Eval results with accept, revise, continue, or stop decisions.
  • Evidence used in operating reviews.
31

Knowledge, context, and operating memory

The business can preserve what it knows and prepare the relevant context for action.

what is known

Capability outcome: facts, definitions, decisions, examples, principles, constraints, standards, and evidence become reusable context for future work.

Typical jobs
  • Maintain specs, SOPs, policies, decision logs, metric definitions, examples, and context packs.
  • Capture operating knowledge from conversations, customer evidence, misses, and repeated work.
  • Prepare job-specific context for people, agents, vendors, and software systems.
  • Remove stale context when the system changes.
Evidence
  • Actors can find the right context when work begins.
  • Fewer repeated explanations and hidden decisions.
  • Artifacts updated when evidence changes.
32

Learning and system improvement

The business can turn misses, discoveries, and wins into a better operating system.

how revision sticks

Capability outcome: the business revises goals, specs, capabilities, jobs, processes, actors, tools, context, evidence, and evaluations when reality teaches something useful.

Typical jobs
  • Detect repeated misses, bottlenecks, defects, unclear work, and new opportunities.
  • Name the missing capability, job, process, action, actor, tool, context, evidence, or evaluation.
  • Run experiments, improvement jobs, retrospectives, and operating reviews.
  • Update the artifact that changes future action.
Evidence
  • Future behavior changes after a lesson.
  • Repeated mistakes decline.
  • New artifacts guide the next actor better than the old system did.

Intake pattern

To use this map with a real business, run the same questions capability by capability:

  1. Outcome: what result must this capability reliably produce?
  2. Current solution: how is the capability handled today?
  3. Jobs: what named bodies of work make the capability exist, operate, or improve?
  4. Actors: who or what performs those jobs now: owner, employee, agent, software, vendor, partner, or parent system?
  5. Process: what steps, decisions, checks, and handoffs make the job repeatable?
  6. Tools and context: what does the actor use, and what must the actor know before acting?
  7. Evidence: what signal proves the outcome happened, and who evaluates it?
  8. Revision: what artifact changes when evidence shows that the capability is weak?

The output is a capability map: each row names the capability, the jobs that support it, the actor model, the current process, the tools and context, the evidence, and the improvement opportunity.

Examples

Product capability across different businesses

Shared capability: create or source a product or service customers want and can buy.

For a gas station, this can mean choosing the right convenience assortment, fuel offer, food program, placement, stock level, freshness standard, and availability. For a medical supply distributor, a large manufacturer may own product design, but the distributor still must solve catalog fit, sourcing, availability, substitutions, compliance attributes, inventory access, pricing, and customer-specific needs.

The capability is the same at the abstract level. The jobs, actors, tools, constraints, and evidence differ by business model.

Payroll as capability and job

Capability: pay people accurately and on time.

Job: run payroll for a pay period. The job may include collecting hours, approving exceptions, calculating pay, submitting payroll, filing taxes, resolving corrections, and recording proof.

The actor could be an owner, internal operator, payroll vendor, software system, agent-assisted workflow, or hybrid. The capability is solved when the outcome happens reliably and evidence is available.

What is settled vs. what is iterating

Settled: a business can be understood as recurring capabilities that produce outcomes through jobs, processes, actors, tools, context, evidence, evaluation, and revision. The same capability may be solved by different actors in different companies.

Iterating: the exact capability taxonomy, the names of the job families under each capability, the intake questions used to populate a real business map, and the evidence standard for judging whether each capability is sufficiently solved.
