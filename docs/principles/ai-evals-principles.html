AI Evals Principles

Durable principles for evaluating AI and agentic systems. These principles apply when Foundation judges whether generated work is acceptable to trust, revise, or promote into reusable substrate.

Format: HTML-native
Scope: AI and agent evaluation
Status: Canonical HTML

Foundation position

AI evals are tests for systems whose correct output is not a single string. Traditional tests still matter, but AI systems need evaluation because the same input can produce many acceptable outputs and many plausible failures.

An eval belongs in Foundation only when it creates verification leverage. The test is useful when it can reject bad output, preserve known-good behavior through change, reveal systematic failures, or improve the system that produced the work.

Generated artifacts need eval principles because their surface shape can pass while their substance fails. A generated spec, plan, design, implementation, report, or decision can look complete while still being too shallow, too ungrounded, too risky, or too ambiguous to become reusable substrate.

Operating rule

Evaluation starts from written intent, operating context, risk tolerance, and observed behavior, then ends in a decision. The evaluator reads traces, artifacts, and evidence; applies the least expensive reliable signal for each claim; and returns a result that can either accept the work or route concrete revisions.

Principles

01
Intent and risk come first
An eval starts from a written contract: what the system should achieve, for whom, in what context, and which failures are unacceptable. Scores without that contract are easy to optimize and hard to trust.
02
Observed behavior precedes measurement
Read traces, outputs, artifacts, and failure examples before writing the eval. A rubric invented before evidence usually measures a theory of failure instead of the system's real failure modes.
03
Outcomes first, traces when conduct matters
Evaluate whether the system completes the intended task in the environment of use. Inspect traces when tool choice, handoffs, side effects, policy compliance, cost, or recovery behavior are part of correctness.
04
The evaluated unit is the whole system
For agents, the model is not the system. Eval records must identify the model, harness, tools, memory, subagents, prompts, run settings, environment, and provider conditions that produced the behavior.
05
Capability is not reliability
A high success score does not prove useful reliability. Evals should separately measure consistency, robustness, predictability, calibration, failure severity, and resource stability when those dimensions affect trust.
06
The environment is part of the eval
Agent evals depend on the sandbox, tools, world state, hidden grader, task definition, and solution isolation. The environment must be production-like enough to matter and constrained enough to prevent reward hacking.
07
Agent failures cascade through steps
An agent can fail at routing, tool choice, arguments, interpretation, synthesis, stopping, or handoff. Evals need enough trace visibility to locate the step where the failure entered.
08
Signals are complementary
Code evals, LLM judges, human review, production traces, and incident reports measure different things. A durable eval system composes them instead of pretending one signal can cover all failure classes.
09
Use the cheapest reliable signal
Deterministic facts should be checked with deterministic code. Semantic claims need semantic judges. Novel, ambiguous, or high-consequence failures need human labeling until the system can encode them.
10
Generation and judgment stay separate
A system should not self-certify important work. The artifact producer, evaluator, rubric, golden examples, and human calibration path should be separable enough that bad work can be rejected without relying on the producer's confidence.
11
Judges are systems under test
An LLM judge is another model-driven component. Its rubric, labels, explanations, bias profile, precision, recall, and agreement with human labels must be evaluated like any other system.
12
Rubrics encode observed distinctions
A good rubric names the role, the evidence, the decision labels, and the failure modes the system has actually shown. It should make disagreement inspectable rather than mysterious.
13
Suites are representative, versioned, and refreshed
Static eval sets drift, saturate, and leak. Durable eval suites preserve version history while adding production failures, edge cases, fresh tasks, and provenance notes that explain what the suite represents.
14
Runs are traceable, reproducible, and auditable
An eval result is weak evidence if it cannot be replayed or explained. Preserve prompts, tools, model versions, datasets, rubrics, traces, artifacts, seeds, settings, and evidence paths needed to compare runs.
15
Robustness and misuse are part of correctness
Happy-path success is incomplete. Evals should cover perturbations, tool errors, long context, unsafe side effects, constraint adherence, misuse pressure, and recovery behavior when those failures matter.
16
Offline and online evals feed each other
Offline datasets compare changes before release. Online traces reveal live failures, drift, and edge cases. The system improves when production failures become new offline cases and offline regressions shape production monitoring.
17
Explanations are diagnostic data
A score alone tells the system what happened. A short explanation tells the system why it happened, which lets failures be grouped into patterns and routed to the right fix.
18
Failures become dataset assets
Today's production failure should become tomorrow's test case. Golden datasets, failure sets, and regression suites are compounding substrate, not disposable QA artifacts.
19
Experiments prove improvement
Prompt, model, tool, and workflow changes should be compared against a known dataset. Improvement means the measured behavior changed in the intended direction without hiding regressions.
20
Reliability is distributional
One good run proves little. Evaluate repeated runs, representative cases, hard cases, known failures, uncertainty, variance, and risk tiers so reliability reflects how the system behaves across a distribution.

Agent implications

Agent evaluation should judge task usefulness, not artifact fluency. The central question is whether a future user or agent could rely on the output to make the next correct move without hidden context.

The eval contract should name intent, context, and risk. The evaluator should know the goal, operating environment, acceptable threshold, and unacceptable failure classes before scoring generated work.

The agent identity must be explicit. A model name is not enough. The run record should identify the harness, tools, subagents, memory state, provider or local inference environment, prompts, seeds, temperature, retries, and other settings needed to reproduce the behavior.

The first pass should inspect the generated artifact on its own terms. Read the generated output before auditing source evidence. This exposes what the artifact itself communicates and prevents the evaluator from mentally filling gaps with outside knowledge.

The evidence pass should sample high-risk claims. An evaluator does not need to reread every source in every pass. It should audit the claims most likely to create durable damage: user-visible behavior, data contracts, permissions, architecture constraints, integrations, safety boundaries, and material discrepancies.

The environment and grader must resist reward hacking. The agent should not see hidden solutions, grader logic, or ungraded task requirements it can ignore. If a task asks for a behavior, the grader must check that behavior or explicitly mark it out of scope.

Run reports should include uncertainty and resource behavior. Success rate, pass@K, error bars or standard error, step count, token use, latency, cost, failure severity, and run-to-run variance are part of the evidence when they affect deployment or trust.

The result should route work, not merely score it. An eval is useful when its findings identify the smallest owning layer that can prevent the failure next time: spec, data, prompt, tool, skill, checker, rubric, implementation, or human decision.

Eval lifecycle

Eval design follows the system lifecycle. Define intent and risk first, choose environment-level outcomes second, calibrate independent judgment third, then maintain representative suites, traceable runs, robustness coverage, and failure-driven substrate updates.

Signal selection

Signal Best for Principle
Code eval Deterministic shape: JSON validity, required fields, path existence, forbidden terms, registry freshness, maximum length, and link integrity. Use code when correctness is mechanically decidable.
LLM judge Semantic quality: intent coverage, faithfulness to source material, user-flow specificity, architecture flexibility, adequacy, tone, and actionability. Use a semantic judge when correctness depends on meaning.
Human review Novel failures, product judgment, ambiguous tradeoffs, golden labels, and acceptance decisions that carry accountability. Use humans to define and calibrate judgment, not to manually repeat scalable checks forever.
Experiment Comparing prompt, model, skill, tool, or rubric changes against stable datasets and known failure sets. Use controlled comparison when the question is whether a change improved the system.
Production trace Live or realistic agent behavior, workflow drift, side effects, unusual inputs, incidents, and failures not represented in the offline suite. Use online evidence to discover new cases; promote recurring failures into versioned offline evals.
Robustness probe Perturbations, tool failures, long-context cases, misuse pressure, and recovery paths that happy-path tests hide. Use robustness probes when failure under stress changes trust, safety, cost, or user outcomes.

Judge quality

Meta-evaluation is required for LLM judges that matter. A judge that gates important work should be tested against labeled examples, known failures, and disagreement cases. The evaluator should track where the judge is too permissive, too strict, position-biased, confidence-biased, self-preferring, or blind to a category of failure.

Judge calibration is a product of examples, labels, and bias checks. Calibrate important judges against golden artifacts, expert labels, pairwise or pass/fail comparisons where useful, and checks for order, length, self-preference, and overconfidence bias.

Human agreement is the benchmark, not perfection. Human reviewers disagree on subjective labels. A judge should be measured against the level of consistency a responsible human process can actually achieve, while still making failures feel fair and explainable.

False positives and false negatives have different costs. In many safety, correctness, and spec-quality gates, reviewing an extra item is cheaper than letting a real failure pass. The eval should make that tradeoff explicit.

A judge should usually return a compact label and a compact explanation. The label enables aggregation. The explanation enables diagnosis, failure clustering, and targeted revision.
