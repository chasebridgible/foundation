Core Principles

Cross-domain principles for building compounding systems in any field. These hold in sport, education, business, operations, software, and AI-assisted work because they describe how foundations, judgment, knowledge, data, feedback, and repeated practice compound over time.

Universal foundations

Foundations reduce future debt. In any serious domain, early technical foundations determine the cost of later progress. In tennis, poor mechanics have to be relearned under pressure and can create injury risk. In organizations, products, teams, and knowledge systems, weak foundations create the same pattern: work appears to move faster early, then every future improvement pays a hidden tax to correct what should have been learned cleanly at the start.

Correct repetition creates leverage; incorrect repetition compounds debt. Practice only compounds when the repeated unit is sound. Repeating a bad motion, a bad sales script, a bad decision process, or a bad operating habit makes the system more confident in the wrong pattern. Feedback turns repetition into learning; repetition without feedback turns mistakes into identity.

Long-term outcomes are path-dependent. Early defaults become constraints. The longer a system operates, the more expensive it becomes to change its base assumptions, vocabulary, incentives, and habits. The foundational move is not to predict every future need; it is to establish primitives that can absorb change without requiring the system to relearn itself.

Compounding and time

Time is either compounding asset value or compounding maintenance liability; there is no neutral default. A system gets more valuable every day it is used, or more painful every day it ages. There is no third option.

Long runs amplify every shortcut. Skipping a rigorous step in a small effort creates a defect; skipping it in a long engagement, training block, operating cycle, or research program creates a recovery project. The longer the run, the more the rigor must be enforced rather than merely suggested.

Reuse across many products, clients, teams, or contexts requires shared core assets, a common means of production, explicit variability management, and management discipline around the asset base. None of those happen by accident. Reuse is an operating choice, enforced by structure.

Knowledge, data, and context

Give durable knowledge a type, an owner, and an audience. When knowledge must survive people, sessions, teams, and tool boundaries, encode it in documents with a single purpose and clear ownership.

  • Each document should have a singular purpose. Mixing audiences creates unusable sprawl.
  • A "knowledge base" only helps if it is curated, scoped, versioned, and written for retrieval.
  • Over-documentation is real; the answer is not "more prose" but better document boundaries and maintenance rules.

Process over prose. Workflows over reference. Steps with exit criteria over essays without them. Reference docs in context produce plausible-sounding generation; workflows produce something that can be done and verified. The same is true for human teams — 200-page handbooks don't get read under time pressure; 40-line workflows with checkpoints do.

Progressive disclosure beats kitchen-sink loading. Every artifact introduced into a working session consumes attention somewhere. Load what is relevant for the current phase; leave the rest organized and retrievable. True for coaching, training, sales, operations, incident response, research, and any work read under time pressure.

Connection, not storage, is the asset. A pile of notes is not a knowledge layer. A knowledge layer is structured so that updating one entity propagates through every related one — so the answer to what's currently going on with this person, client, deal, or project is always one query, not a manual scan across files. The defensible thing is the graph between entries, not the entries themselves. (After Garry Tan: the difference between a filing cabinet and a nervous system is connection, not storage.)

Data value increases with time when labels preserve context. Data is not just a pile of observations; it is an accumulating record of decisions, outcomes, edge cases, and behavior under real conditions. The same event is cheap to label at capture and expensive to reconstruct later. Systems that collect clean, consistently labeled data naturally become compounding systems because every future analysis, audit, training cycle, and decision has more evidence to work from. Unlabeled data still accumulates, but it compounds ambiguity instead of leverage.

Context is the moat. In any system that delegates work to people, vendors, software, or AI, the captured operating mind of the organization is the defensible asset: what it offers, who it serves, what it promises, who decides what, what the constraints are, and how judgment is applied. Labor can often be replaced; operating context cannot. Two teams with the same tools will diverge based on the quality of the context they have captured.

Intent and execution

Separate intent from implementation. What you want is not the same as how it gets built. Specifying both well, and keeping them clearly distinguished, is most of the work.

As capability gets cheaper, articulated intent does not. The bottleneck moves from can the work be done to can the responsible person describe what should be done well enough that the executor knows when it is done. The leverage move is getting good at articulating intent.

Write down the done-condition before the work starts. A contract — testable, external, agreed in advance — is the single highest-leverage move against scope drift. Once work is underway, "done" can be quietly redefined; pre-writing the exit criterion refuses that move.

Scope discipline: touch only what the goal requires. The single biggest determinant of whether work is acceptable on first pass or has to be unwound is whether it stayed inside the agreed intent. A change that does three extra things may look helpful, but it makes review, learning, and accountability harder.

Pre-write the rebuttals. People and tools are excellent at rationalizing skipped rigor. Anti-rationalization tables pair common excuses ("this is too simple to define," "I'll measure it later," "good enough, move on") with a written rebuttal. You are refusing to argue with the executor, or your tired future self, on the day by writing the argument down before it happens.

Verification and feedback

Execution
Produces candidate work
A person, team, or tool creates the artifact. Output quality is not self-certifying.
Evaluation
Supplies the exit signal
Measurements, reviewers, judges, tests, scoreboards, or observed outcomes decide whether the work matches intent.

Verification is the load-bearing exit criterion. Every workflow terminates in concrete evidence: a measured outcome, a coach's review, a customer response, an audit trail, a working demonstration, a passing test, or a signed acceptance. The executor produces candidate work; a separate signal decides whether the work is done. "Seems right" never closes the loop.

Separate execution from evaluation. The producer of work has a natural bias toward accepting it. Independent evaluation is not stylistic overhead; it is what prevents confident output from replacing verified progress. In human systems this can be a coach, reviewer, market signal, or measurement. In AI systems this becomes an explicit generator/evaluator or worker/judge split.

Simplicity and clarity

Choose the simplest, most boring design whose failure modes you understand, and do not dismantle load-bearing weirdness until you have captured its behavior.

  • Design should optimize for easy future change, not momentary cleverness.
  • If a behavior astonishes its users, redesign it.
  • Before changing behavior you do not understand, capture the current behavior with observable evidence.

Prefer the boring, obvious solution. Cleverness is expensive. The executor is not penalized for picking the more interesting path; the system is. Default to the dullest design whose failure modes you understand and reach for cleverness only when the boring version provably will not do.

Human judgment and delegation

Posture matters more than tool. The same tool, coach, teammate, contractor, or model can hollow out one person's mental model and sharpen another's, depending on whether it is used to think or instead of thinking. Cognitive offloading delegates the how while keeping the what; cognitive surrender lets another party's output become yours by default. They can look identical from the outside.

Borrowed confidence is the failure mode. Fluent outputs, expert language, senior titles, dashboards, and models all create confidence that can transfer faster than reasoning. Confidence transfers cleanly; judgment does not.

Calibration is the skill. The job is to know, in any given moment, whether delegation is helping you think or quietly doing the thinking for you. If output is increasing and understanding is shrinking, you are paying with comprehension debt. If output is increasing and understanding is growing, the system is extending your judgment.

Cooperation builds mental models; blind delegation erodes them. With cooperation, the responsible person ends the session with a sharper model of the problem than they started. With blind delegation, the executor does. Delegated labor should be the second mind in the room, not the only one.

Cognitive surrender is path-dependent; comprehension debt compounds like any other debt. Each accepted piece of work you do not understand makes the next change to that work more likely to be another act of surrender, because forming an independent view now requires reconstructing what you skipped. The bill comes due when something breaks and no one can rebuild the system from first principles.

Compounding systems and substrate

A compounding system has five layers, each with a different durability profile. Substrate is the durable layer: principles, examples, playbooks, data, templates, reusable assets, and operating memory. Iterating layers are the tools, tactics, vendors, and temporary methods that change on someone else's cadence. Operational shape is who does what. Intake layers are where work originates: human input, monitoring, feedback, customer signals, or ambient observation. The delivery loop is the contract that turns intake into shipped work and substrate updates. Investments in each layer are not interchangeable; the discipline is knowing which layer you are investing in. See compounding-system.md.

Distinguish substrate from iterating layers. The substrate is what lets a future operator, with better tools and more experience, rebuild a better version of the system from durable artifacts. Iterating layers are everything else: useful, sometimes necessary, but tied to present conditions. The discipline is knowing which one you are working on and budgeting time accordingly.

Prefer building substrate to building iterating layers. Time spent reproducing what the market, a tool vendor, or a changing tactic will soon commoditize is rented leverage. It can buy short-term advantage; it cannot compound into a long-term moat unless the work leaves behind reusable knowledge, data, examples, or operating assets.

Substrate compounds with use; iterating layers age with use. Every improvement to substrate pays out across future work. Every improvement to a current-tool-specific tactic mostly defends against drift. Both have to happen. Only one of them earns its keep over time.

Substrate grows through codification, not accumulation. Ad-hoc work does not become substrate by itself. Either the same workflow is reinvented every time, or someone rewrites it as a reusable skill, capability, template, curriculum, checklist, or SOP. The meta-skill (Garry Tan calls it skillify) is the explicit move that promotes a one-off into permanent substrate: examine recent work, extract the repeatable pattern, name the triggers and edge cases, write it as a versioned artifact, and register it where the system can find it next time. Without that move, workflows accumulate; with it, they compound.

The operating loop

When a step fails, go back to the smallest correction — usually the spec. Don't restart from scratch. Update the smallest upstream artifact that is wrong, regenerate the downstream artifacts that depend on it, re-run. The loop is the recovery mechanism, not just the build mechanism.

The loop is recursive; the steps are the contract. A large goal contains smaller goals, each running its own cycle. The executor is free to break a top-level goal into any number of inner loops, in any order, using whatever methods fit. The steps of the loop are the contract; the structure inside them is open.
